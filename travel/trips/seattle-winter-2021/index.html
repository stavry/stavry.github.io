Seattle (Winter 2021)

2021-12-08
6 minute read
trip report
travel

12.08.21
It’s an overcast day in the Bay, rare for any time of year. Fortuneately we’re heading to a place famous for it’s unrelenting cloud cover! Ha! We depart early morning from Palo Alto, headed for the San Jose airport. The flight is uneventful save for the descent - the plane shoots north past Seattle (did we get on the wrong flight? I don’t want to go to Canada), turning around eventually in a large arc before descending over the city into SeaTac. We had planned to take an Uber from the airport into the city, but as it turns out, the monorail (Link) is much cheaper ($2!), so we opt for that instead, meandering lazily through the Seattle suburbs with stops few and far between.

We arrive in Capitol Hill a few hours before check-in - time to explore. It’s a good thing we chose to travel light (one Gregory day pack each - a holdover from the Half Dome hike), since we’re on our feet for the next few hours. The first stop is Tacos Chukis. It’s right by the Capitol Hill Link station and we imagine it’ll hit the flavor spot after a morning of travel. This turns out to be a correct assumption - in fact all of the meals we’ll have in the coming days will be fantastic. We order a few of the eponymous tacos (al pastor!) and settle in for a bit.

Afterwards, we stroll south, through Cal Anderson park, looking for somewhere/something to kill the next few hours. The ground is wet, covered in the perpetual shiny slick of the Pacific Northwest, and the paths have disintegrated into muddy streams. This can be good or bad - I guess it all depends on your outlook. I go through phases of loving/hating the everpresent moisture. Despite all this (or maybe it’s a relatively good day?), there are people everywhere - even playing basketball in the muck. I guess when you live here, you have to take advantage of whatever weather you can - a fact I am familiar with having lived in alternately humid/freezing Nashville most of my life.

Never ones to leave a bookstore unexplored, we pop into the Elliott Bay Book Company, just south of the park. It’s no Powell’s, but it’s a welcome intellectual reprieve nonetheless. I gravitate towards the colorful smorgasbord of magazines - there are so many for so many interesting subjects - Cycling, Climbing, Literature, all sorts of visual art. I wish these sorts of magazines were available everywhere, but I seldom see them outside of these sorts of bookstores.

By this point, the packs are beginning to feel cumbersome, so it comes as a relief when we get a message that our AirBnB is ready early. Eagerly, we head back out, crossing through the lively center of Pike/Pine and its lovely array of coffeeshops/eateries/bars/tchotchke sellers.

A curious thing about Seattle - for a city with so much rain, the umbrellas are noticeably absent. The key to this riddle is the prevalence of beanies everywhere. With rain a gentle patter most of the time, beanies suffice to keep heads dry and arms unencumbered. So, after settling in a bit and warming up a bit too much, we head out again for the night, making a direct beeline for the Carhartt store so we too can stock up on the essential beanie.

Downtown is aglow with Christmas lights, installations draped high across wide downtown avenues and occupying plazas like pieces in an outdoor museum, providing their own lighting. Crowds mill and gather cheerily taking pictures with the lights, a welcome sight after a long, dreary pandemic winter. The image we use on this website for our logo emerges from this outing.

We continue deeper into South Lake Union, headed for the Amazon spheres, under which there is supposedly a cocktail bar recommended by a friend. The spheres (Jeff Bezos' balls?) form a lattice enclosure, an oversized terrarium brimming with artificially supported plant life in the middle of winter. Outside is a row of seesaws which sound and chime at each descent, gimmicky, but fun nonetheless.

Afterwards, we circle around the spheres and finally run into Deep Dive, a recessed cocktail bar hidden between two folds at the base of the spheres. The atmosphere can best be described as dark - small taxidermied wildlife (squirrels, rabbits) intricately arrayed in individual bell jars, large ore deposits under tasteful dim lights - it feels like we’ve chanced upon an underground mining operation, albeit one without the requisite fine dust coating the air.

The cocktails are expertly prepared behind an arcing, polished wooden bar. Brass instruments, a large bowl of semi-peeled citrus, and small glass bottles filled with multicolored liqueurs form the accoutrements specific to this establishment, and there is of course the backlit shelf of rare liquors (ancient potions literally glowing with implied power) forming the back wall of the bar with more mining and taxidermy ephemera dotted throughout.

It’s nice, but we prefer places where there isn’t any sociological separation between patrons and proprietors. We’ve never believed much in service (I can get my own food and drinks, and most of the time I’d rather cook it myself too), especially in the servile sense in which it usually employed at supposedly higher end places. The drinks are delicious of course (a gin fizz with a yuzu twist and ???), with a healthy side of freshly baked foccacia. Overall it’s an enjoyable way to whet the appetite, but we leave yearning for a bar where we can actually have a conversation with the bartender (what is Seattle like now, what has it been in the past, where is the energy and life which attracts and keeps people in cities in the first place?).

The final leg of the night is to the Malaysian restaurant Kedai Makan, a straight shot across I-5 and back into Capitol Hill. The Bay Area has a rich tradition in providing excellent forms of the predominant Asian cuisines (Indian, Chinese, Japanese, Korean), but Seattle seems to excel in the Southeast Asian diaspora (Vietnamese, Lao, Malay, Indonesian), an exciting discovery.

The most exciting part of the walk is when we’re on one of the bridges which pass over I-5 in downtown Seattle like makeshift arteries hanging precariously over the violence of the highway. Growing up in Nashville, I recall the countless walks I took after school (mostly through rain and cold), beginning at one end in downtown Nashville (where I went to high school), and crossing over the busy I-40 into uptown and to Vanderbilt (where my parents worked). Standing on that bridge transported me back to those scenes in my memory, and I couldn’t help but feel that Seattle so far had felt like equal parts Berkeley and Nashville, the two places I’ve spent most of my life. It’s nice at moments like these to feel like you’re in a familiar place, even so far away from home.

We arrive at Kedai Makan, but it’s packed (a welcome sight), and with the chill outside, we decide to get our food to go. Walking back through a new throughfare in Capitol Hill, we make plans for the coming days, entranced by the energy emanating from the late night menagerie.
